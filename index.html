Indian Art Form

Warli art is a traditional folk art from the Warli tribe in Maharashtra, India, characterized by its simplicity and use of basic geometric shapes like circles, triangles, and lines. It depicts everyday village life, rituals, and nature, often on mud walls of homes. The art form gained recognition in the 1970s, particularly through the work of artist Jivya Soma Mashe.

Madhubani art (also known as Mithila art) is a style of painting originating in the Mithila region, which spans across the Indian states of Bihar and West Bengal, as well as parts of southern Nepal. Janakpurdham, Ranchi and Rasidpur are the three most notable cities associated with the tradition and evolution of Mithila art. The art was traditionally practiced by female members. Artists create these paintings using a variety of mediums, including their own fingers, or twigs, brushes, nib-pens, and matchsticks. The paint is created using natural dyes and pigments such as ochre and lampblack are used for reddish brown and black respectively.

Dhokra art is a traditional Indian metal casting technique using the lost-wax method, predominantly found in states like Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, West Bengal, and Odisha. It's known for its unique, folk-inspired motifs and sculptures, often featuring animals, mythical creatures, and human figures, according to ExclusiveLane and India Currents. The craft is a living tradition practiced by various artisan communities, passed down through generations.

Pyrography, also known as wood burning, is an art form where designs are created on wood or other materials by using a heated tool to burn lines and patterns. This technique allows for a wide range of artistic expression, from intricate details to more abstract designs. Pyrography has a rich history and is practiced in various cultures around the world.
